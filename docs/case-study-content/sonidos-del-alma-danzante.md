# Sonidos del Alma Danzante
## Sounds of the Dancing Souls

Building a full brand identity for a Cumbia music festival rooted in East Los Angeles, from naming and research through logo, brand system, and a co-branded festival kit.

---

**Timeline:** Feb. – June 2023
**Course:** DES 116 (Brand Identity) + DES 144 (Co-Branding), UC Davis
**Type:** Academic Project
**Role:** Solo Designer, Festival Director
**Tools:** Figma, InDesign, Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop
**Disciplines:** Branding, Graphic Design, Marketing, Event Planning

---

### Overview

Sonidos del Alma Danzante, which translates to "Sounds of the Dancing Souls," is a conceptual Cumbia music festival set in East Los Angeles. The project was part of UC Davis's DES 116 course, where students were tasked with building a full brand identity for an event from the ground up: naming, research, creative brief, logo, brand guidelines, and marketing materials. A second course, DES 144, extended the project into co-branding territory.

East Los Angeles is one of the most predominantly Latino communities in the country, with some neighborhoods exceeding 96% Latino population. Cumbia, a genre born on Colombia's Caribbean coast and now deeply woven into Mexican and Latin American identity, felt like the right fit for the place and the people.

This project was personal. I'm Mexican. I wanted to do it right.

---

### The Brief

The festival had a clear purpose: give emerging and established Cumbia artists a platform to perform, gain exposure, and celebrate the culture with their community. It would also open the door for cross-cultural exchange with audiences outside the Hispanic/Latino community.

**Deliverables:**
- Festival name and logo
- Brand guidelines
- Advertising (posters, digital posts)
- Event signage
- Merchandise
- Co-branded festival kit

**Budget:** $200,000 (hypothetical)
**Location:** East Los Angeles, CA
**Tone:** Energetic, lively, and engaging. Community-driven, authentic, and hectic.

### Research

Before sketching anything, I spent time understanding what Cumbia actually is and where it comes from. Not just surface-level, but the real history.

Key things I learned:

- Cumbia originated on Colombia's Caribbean coast as an African courtship dance, blending African, Indigenous, and European influences over centuries
- Traditional instruments include the tambora, llamador, tambor alegre, maracas, and gaita flutes. Modern cumbia adds bass, piano, brass, congas, and timbales.
- By the 1940s, cumbia had arrived in Mexico. Artists like Luis Carlos Meyer Castandet and Rafael de Paz helped popularize it, blending it with mariachi and Caribbean styles.
- Cumbia evolved into distinct regional subgenres: cumbia sonidera (Mexico), chicha (Peru), cumbia norteña, cumbia andina, and more.
- Cumbia sonidera, the subgenre most relevant to the LA context, grew out of an underground DJ scene in Mexico City neighborhoods like Peñón de los Baños and Tepito before spreading widely.

I also looked at the demographic reality of the location. East Los Angeles is 96.7% Latino. Maywood is 96.4%. Boyle Heights is 94.0%. This wasn't a festival being parachuted into a neighborhood. It was meant to belong to it.

**Visual research** included imagery from Mexican pottery, Aztec tiles, traditional dresses, musicians, dancers, and the visual language of East LA itself.

### Naming

I went through ten potential names before landing on the final one:

- Cumbia Primavera
- Noche de Cumbia Mística
- Cumbia Mistica LA
- Mística Ritmos Cumbia
- Cumbia Rítmica
- Sonidos Cumbieros
- Cumbia en el Aire
- Sonidos del Alma
- **Sonidos del Alma Danzante**
- Vibraciones del Alma

"Sonidos del Alma Danzante" felt complete. It captured movement, soul, and sound in a single phrase. "Sounds of the Dancing Souls" was evocative without being literal, and it carried the spiritual energy that Cumbia, at its best, actually produces on a dance floor.

---

### Logo Design

The logo was where I got the most stuck. I went through many, many variations before landing on the final direction. Getting the letterforms, the weight, and the cultural feeling to all work together took longer than any other part of this project.

The final logo balances a bold, hand-lettered quality that nods to the expressiveness of Latin design traditions with enough structure to work across all applications. Logo variants were developed for different backgrounds and use cases, with clearspace rules tied to the width of the "DEL" in the wordmark.

Logo system included:
- Primary logo
- Secondary logo
- Third logo variation
- Clearspace rules
- Logo don'ts (no skewing, no unapproved colors, no placement directly on images, no axis flips)

### Brand System

**Color Palette**

Three core colors, each chosen to reflect something real about the festival:

- **Fiesta Flame (#E55239):** A fiery red-orange representing the raw energy and heat of Cumbia on a dance floor. Primary color at 60% use.
- **Sunlit Sand (#FFE5B8):** A warm golden tone evoking the lively, open atmosphere of the festival and the sun-soaked character of East LA. Secondary at 30% use.
- **Tropical Turquoise (#038D84):** A deep teal representing depth and cultural diversity. Accent color at 10% use.

A neutral system (off-white, light silver, dark gray, carbon gray) was defined for supporting applications.

**Typography**

- **Lemon Milk:** Display and headline font. Wide, bold, all-caps character that commands attention and reads as energetic without being chaotic.
- **Plus Jakarta Sans:** Body and supporting text. Clean and legible, balances the expressiveness of Lemon Milk.

Full type hierarchy was established across eight levels from Headline 1 down to Overline, with specific sizes, weights, spacing, and line heights.

**Visual Elements**

Custom illustrations were created for the brand, drawing from the visual language of Cumbia culture: movement, energy, community, and color. Image guidelines were established for photography used alongside the brand.

**Marketing Materials**

Both digital and physical marketing applications were designed, including posters, digital posts, and event signage.

### Co-Branding: Jarritos

For DES 144, the project extended into co-branding. I chose Jarritos.

That was an intentional decision, not a random one. Jarritos is one of the most recognizable Mexican soft drink brands in the world. Founded in 1950, it's become an icon of Mexican culture and shows up at quinceañeras, family gatherings, street food stands, and community events. It's not just a soda. It's part of the cultural fabric.

Pairing Sonidos del Alma Danzante with Jarritos felt honest. Both celebrate Mexican and Latin culture. Both belong in East LA. The co-branded festival kit covered three categories: beverages, food, and clothing, with the two brands sharing visual space naturally because their personalities and values were already aligned.

### Reflection

This project taught me that research isn't just preparation for design. It is part of the design.

Understanding Cumbia's history, how it traveled from Colombia's Caribbean coast through Mexico and Peru and into the streets of East LA, informed every visual decision I made. The colors, the energy, the name, the choice of Jarritos as a co-brand partner. None of those were arbitrary. They came from actually learning what the culture is and what it means to the people who live it.

But the honest reflection is that I could have gone deeper. I'm Mexican. This is my culture. And I still felt like I was only scratching the surface. Jarritos and the colors are real parts of the culture, but what actually makes the culture the culture is something harder to put on a poster: the sense of community, the way music creates belonging, the fact that Cumbia isn't just listened to, it's danced to, together, in circles. I captured some of that. I think with more time and more research I could have captured more.

The logo struggle was also a real lesson. I went through more variations on that wordmark than anything else in this project. Looking back, that process was necessary, even when it felt frustrating. The final logo only works because I exhausted what didn't work first.

If I did this again, I'd spend the first week doing nothing but talking to people in East LA about what this festival would mean to them before I ever opened Illustrator.
